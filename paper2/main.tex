% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
We introduce \textbf{\datasetname}, a 70{,}000-image benchmark for
AI-generated image detection comprising 10{,}000 real photographs
paired with 60{,}000 synthetic images produced by six architecturally
diverse text-to-image diffusion models: Stable Diffusion~1.5, SDXL,
FLUX.1-schnell, Kandinsky~2.2, PixArt-$\Sigma$, and W\"{u}rstchen.
Unlike prior benchmarks, \textbf{\datasetname} applies a single
\emph{canonical preprocessing pipeline}---JPEG equalisation followed
by lossless PNG serialisation---identically to both real and AI
images, structurally eliminating the JPEG compression and resolution
asymmetries that allow detectors to exploit format shortcuts rather
than synthesis artefacts.
All synthetic images are generated from BLIP-2 captions derived
directly from the real photographs, holding semantic content constant
across classes, and splits are assigned at the pair level so that no
real image and its six AI counterparts appear in different folds.
A canonical-format audit confirms zero violations across all
70{,}000 images.
A one-epoch ResNet-18 sniff test yields an overall detectability score
of 0.860, with per-generator scores ranging from 0.843 (W\"{u}rstchen,
healthy zone) to 0.973 (SDXL, caution zone), establishing that
elevated scores reflect genuine synthesis fingerprints rather than
residual format artefacts.
Three baseline detectors are evaluated on the held-out test split:
EfficientNet-B4 achieves an AUROC of 0.9807, while a leave-one-out
cross-generator experiment reveals a consistent architectural divide---
detectors generalise poorly to held-out UNet generators
(AUROC~$\approx 0.86$--$0.89$) but near-perfectly to held-out
non-UNet generators (AUROC~$\approx 0.99$).
This gap is invisible to single-family datasets and has direct
implications for future benchmark design.
The complete dataset, preprocessing library, generation notebooks,
and baseline checkpoints are publicly available at
\url{https://huggingface.co/datasets/Shanmuk4622/ai-detection-dataset-v2}.
\end{abstract}

% ── §I  Introduction ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The capability of text-to-image generative models to produce
photorealistic imagery from natural language descriptions has advanced
dramatically over the past three years.
Open-weight models such as Stable Diffusion~\cite{rombach2022ldm},
SDXL~\cite{podell2024sdxl}, and FLUX~\cite{flux2024bfl} have reduced
the marginal cost of synthetic image creation to near zero, enabling
a wide range of beneficial applications in art, design, and
accessibility while simultaneously expanding the threat surface for
disinformation, identity fraud, and manipulated media at scale.
The urgency of reliable automated detection has grown in direct
proportion: a recent comprehensive evaluation of open-source detectors
found that mean detection accuracy against 2024-era generators has
fallen to approximately 38\%, down from around 79\% against generators
from 2020--2021~\cite{bai2026opendetect}, illustrating an accelerating
arms race in which synthesis capabilities consistently outpace
detection.

Constructing a detection benchmark that is genuinely useful for this
problem is harder than it appears.
Two structural biases, identified across widely used prior benchmarks,
systematically inflate reported detection numbers without producing
detectors that generalise to real-world deployment conditions.
The first is a \emph{JPEG compression disparity}: real images sourced
from the web are typically stored as JPEG with quality factors around
96, whereas generated images are saved as lossless PNG.
Detectors trained on such data learn to distinguish classes primarily
by measuring DCT coefficient distributions---a format shortcut with
no connection to synthesis artefacts and no robustness to routine
post-processing~\cite{grommelt2024jpeg}.
Grommelt~\etal~\cite{grommelt2024jpeg} demonstrated that this
single bias inflates reported cross-generator detection by up to
11~percentage points on GenImage~\cite{zhu2023genimage}.
The second is a \emph{content distribution mismatch}: when generation
prompts are chosen independently of the real images, detectors may
exploit systematic differences in scene composition, object frequency,
or caption vocabulary, rather than visual synthesis characteristics,
between the two classes.

This paper addresses both problems simultaneously through three
deliberate design choices.
First, every image---real or AI---passes through an identical
canonical preprocessing function that equalises JPEG compression
history and strips all metadata, making format-based shortcuts
structurally impossible by construction.
Second, all synthetic images are generated from BLIP-2
captions~\cite{li2023blip2} derived directly from the real
photographs, so the semantic content available to every generator
is held constant across classes.
Third, splits are assigned at the \emph{pair level}: a real image
and all six of its AI counterparts always appear in the same fold,
preventing prompt content or scene composition from leaking across
the split boundary.
Figure~\ref{fig:pairing} illustrates the pairing and preprocessing
concept.

The resulting dataset, \textbf{\datasetname}, contains 70{,}000
images spanning six generators that collectively cover the three
principal backbone families in modern text-to-image synthesis:
UNet latent diffusion models~\cite{rombach2022ldm,podell2024sdxl},
attention-based Diffusion Transformers~\cite{chen2024pixart,flux2024bfl},
and hierarchical two-stage
architectures~\cite{razzhigaev2023kandinsky,pernias2023wuerstchen}.
This architectural breadth is a deliberate contribution: prior paired
datasets~\cite{jiang2024passive} cover at most four generators from
a single backbone family, making cross-family generalisation
measurements structurally impossible.
Our experiments confirm that this architectural diversity reveals a
generalisation asymmetry---the UNet-vs-non-UNet gap---that is
invisible to single-family benchmarks.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.2em, itemsep=2pt]
\item A \textbf{70{,}000-image paired dataset} (10{,}000 real
  photographs~$\times$~7, one per class) in which real and AI
  images share identical canonical preprocessing, caption-grounded
  semantic content, and deterministic pair-level splits across six
  architecturally diverse generators.
\item A \textbf{leak-free canonical preprocessing pipeline}
  (Algorithm~\ref{alg:preprocess}) that provably eliminates JPEG
  compression and resolution asymmetries; verified by a
  canonical-format audit finding zero violations across all
  70{,}000 images.
\item \textbf{Baseline detector results} for
  ResNet-50~\cite{he2016resnet},
  EfficientNet-B4~\cite{tan2019efficientnet}, and
  ViT-B/16~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit}, including per-generator
  binary AUROC breakdown and a six-way leave-one-out cross-generator
  generalisation experiment.
\item \textbf{Empirical evidence of a UNet-vs-non-UNet
  generalisation gap}: detectors trained on non-UNet generators
  generalise poorly to held-out UNet architectures
  (AUROC~$\approx 0.86$--$0.89$) while detectors trained on UNet
  generators generalise near-perfectly to held-out non-UNet
  architectures (AUROC~$\approx 0.99$), with direct implications
  for how future detection benchmarks should be structured.
\item \textbf{An ablation analysis} validating each of the three
  core design choices---JPEG equalisation, content grounding, and
  pair-level splitting---against literature evidence and internal
  audit results.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Why EfficientNet-B4 Outperforms ResNet-50}
\label{ssec:disc_resnet}

The most striking result in Table~\ref{tab:baselines} is the AUROC
gap between ResNet-50 (0.7032) and EfficientNet-B4 (0.9807) despite
both models being trained under identical hyperparameters.
The gap is not attributable to parameter count: EfficientNet-B4 has
approximately 19~million parameters versus ResNet-50's 25~million,
yet achieves a substantially higher separation between classes.

The explanation lies in effective receptive field size and
feature hierarchy.
ResNet-50's $3 \times 3$ convolutions are well-suited for capturing
local texture patterns but cannot model the long-range spatial
correlations that characterise diffusion model synthesis artefacts.
Modern diffusion models introduce subtle high-frequency patterns
in semantically flat regions (\eg, skies, walls, bodies of water)
through the iterative denoising process~\cite{corvi2023detection};
these patterns are distributed non-locally across the image rather
than being concentrated at any single spatial scale.
EfficientNet-B4's compound scaling---simultaneously increasing
network width, depth, and input resolution---provides substantially
larger effective receptive fields, enabling the model to integrate
spatially extended artefact signals across all six generator families.

The ViT-B/16 result is instructive in a different way: despite
achieving accuracy (0.8744) comparable to ResNet-50 (0.8757), its
AUROC (0.8125 versus 0.7032) is substantially higher.
This AUROC gap without a corresponding accuracy gap indicates that
the ViT produces better-calibrated class probability estimates even
where its discrete classification decisions are similar to those of
ResNet-50.
This is consistent with attention-based feature extraction capturing
longer-range spatial context than local convolutions, and suggests
that the ViT's ranking of images by confidence is more reliable than
ResNet-50's---an important property for detection systems that
operate at varying confidence thresholds.

\subsection{W\"{u}rstchen and the Detectability Gradient}
\label{ssec:disc_wuerstchen}

W\"{u}rstchen presents an unusual and informative detectability
profile.
It is the only generator to fall in the \emph{healthy} sniff-test
zone (0.843), indicating that a single training epoch is insufficient
for a shallow network to reliably extract its artefact signature.
Yet the fully trained EfficientNet-B4 achieves AUROC~$= 0.9745$
on W\"{u}rstchen images, confirming that the artefacts are
learnable given sufficient training.
Most strikingly, in the leave-one-out cross-generator experiment,
W\"{u}rstchen achieves a cross-gen AUROC of 0.992 despite never
having been seen during training---comparable to
Kandinsky~2.2 (0.993) and PixArt-$\Sigma$ (0.993).

This combination---hard to detect in one epoch, yet near-trivially
detectable when held out---can be explained through artefact
distinctiveness.
W\"{u}rstchen's 42$\times$ spatial compression produces frequency
profiles qualitatively unlike those of any UNet-based generator.
When a detector trained exclusively on UNet and Transformer
generators encounters a W\"{u}rstchen image, it has no matching
template in its learned representation, making the image read as
anomalous.
This \emph{anomaly-detection} mechanism---``this image resembles
none of the generators I have learned''---is qualitatively different
from the \emph{feature-matching} mechanism that operates within the
UNet family.
The single-epoch probe is weak precisely because anomaly detection
requires a stable model of the in-distribution generators, which is
not available after just one training epoch.

\subsection{The UNet-vs-Non-UNet Generalisation Gap}
\label{ssec:disc_crossgen}

The leave-one-out experiment (Table~\ref{tab:cross_gen}) reveals
a clean architectural boundary in generalisation difficulty.
UNet-based generators---SD~1.5 (AUROC~$= 0.890$) and SDXL
(AUROC~$= 0.861$)---are substantially harder to detect when held
out, with F1 scores of 0.667 and 0.657 respectively, below the
0.70 threshold one might consider practically useful.
By contrast, non-UNet generators---W\"{u}rstchen (0.992),
Kandinsky~2.2 (0.993), and PixArt-$\Sigma$ (0.993)---are
near-perfectly detectable even without direct training exposure.

Two mechanisms drive this asymmetry.
First, \emph{within-family artefact similarity}: SD~1.5 and SDXL
share the same UNet backbone architecture and CLIP-conditioned
latent space, producing synthesis artefacts that are similar enough
in structure that a detector trained on one UNet generator already
partially explains another.
This partial coverage is not sufficient for high-precision detection
(F1 below 0.67), but it means the held-out UNet is not
\emph{completely} unseen to the model's learned representation.
Second, \emph{cross-family distinctiveness}: Transformer and
hierarchical architectures introduce qualitatively different
frequency profiles~\cite{corvi2023detection}, making their outputs
highly distinct from both real images and UNet outputs.
A detector trained on UNet generators effectively learns ``what
AI-generated UNet images look like,'' and any image that strongly
deviates from both real images and this learned template is flagged
as AI-generated, regardless of which non-UNet architecture produced it.

This asymmetry has direct implications for benchmark design.
A dataset containing only UNet-based generators---which describes
most existing benchmarks---will systematically overestimate
cross-generator generalisation because it measures within-family
transfer rather than across-family transfer.
Conversely, a dataset containing only non-UNet generators will
underestimate the challenge, because held-out non-UNet generators
are near-trivially detectable through the anomaly mechanism.
\textbf{\datasetname}'s coverage of all three backbone families is
necessary to reveal this asymmetry cleanly.

\subsection{FLUX.1-schnell: A Middle Position}
\label{ssec:disc_flux}

FLUX.1-schnell occupies an interesting intermediate position in the
cross-generator results (AUROC~$= 0.959$, F1~$= 0.869$).
As a rectified flow Transformer, it belongs to the same broad
non-UNet family as PixArt-$\Sigma$, yet its cross-generator
detectability is notably lower (0.959 versus 0.993).
This may reflect FLUX's few-step distillation regime, which
produces images with fewer iterative sampling artefacts than
conventional diffusion models.
The four-step inference schedule used here produces images that, in
frequency space, may be closer to real photographs than the outputs
of SDXL at eight steps or PixArt-$\Sigma$ at twenty steps,
reducing the strength of the out-of-distribution signal that drives
the anomaly-detection mechanism for non-UNet generators.
This interpretation is consistent with the general finding of
Bai~\etal~\cite{bai2026opendetect} that detection accuracy is lower
for more recent models.

\subsection{Limitations and Open Problems}
\label{ssec:disc_limits}

\paragraph{Scale.}
At 70{,}000 images, \textbf{\datasetname} is smaller than
GenImage~\cite{zhu2023genimage} ($1.3$~million) or
AI-GenBench~\cite{pellegrini2025aigenbench} (310{,}000).
The trade-off was deliberate: preprocessing integrity and content
matching take priority over raw scale because, as Grommelt~\etal\
\cite{grommelt2024jpeg} demonstrated, preprocessing shortcuts in
large-scale datasets can dominate the detection signal.
Future work should explore whether the pipeline scales efficiently
to larger corpora while preserving the canonical-format guarantee.

\paragraph{Text-to-image scope.}
The dataset covers text-to-image generation only.
Edit-based synthesis (\eg, InstructPix2Pix, IP-Adapter) and
reference-conditioned generation produce different artefact
profiles that may require separate evaluation frameworks.
Detectors fine-tuned on \textbf{\datasetname} should not be assumed
to generalise to these modalities without re-evaluation.

\paragraph{Inference hyperparameters.}
Step counts reflect real-world fast-inference usage: SDXL at 8
steps, FLUX.1-schnell at 4 steps, W\"{u}rstchen at 20/10 stages.
Images generated at higher step counts may carry different
artefact profiles~\cite{corvi2023detection}, and detectors trained
on fast-inference outputs may not generalise to high-quality,
high-step outputs from the same generator.

\paragraph{Single captioner and vocabulary.}
All 10{,}000 prompts were generated by BLIP-2
(\texttt{blip2-opt-2.7b})~\cite{li2023blip2} and share a common
vocabulary and sentence structure.
Human-authored or multi-captioner prompts would increase lexical
diversity and reduce the risk of caption-style artifacts becoming
a latent training signal.

\paragraph{Cross-architecture detection limits.}
The F1 scores for held-out SD~1.5 (0.667) and SDXL (0.657) suggest
that cross-family UNet detection remains a substantially unsolved
problem.
These low F1 scores imply that a deployed detector trained on a
typical mix of non-UNet generators would produce high false-negative
rates for the most widely deployed open-weight generators, which are
precisely the ones most likely to be used in adversarial settings.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \textbf{\datasetname}, a 70{,}000-image paired dataset
for AI-generated image detection built around two structural
principles that most existing benchmarks violate: a canonical
preprocessing pipeline applied identically to real and AI images,
and caption-grounded semantic matching that eliminates content
distribution differences between classes.
Across six generators spanning UNet, Diffusion Transformer, and
hierarchical backbone families, EfficientNet-B4 achieves an AUROC
of 0.9807 under provably bias-free conditions.

The cross-generator leave-one-out experiment revealed a consistent
and previously unmeasured architectural divide: detectors generalise
poorly to unseen UNet generators (AUROC~$\approx 0.86$--$0.89$)
while generalising near-perfectly to unseen non-UNet generators
(AUROC~$\approx 0.99$).
This gap, which arises from artefact similarity within the UNet
family and artefact distinctiveness of non-UNet architectures, is
structurally invisible to single-family benchmarks and has direct
consequences for how detection systems should be trained and evaluated
as new generative architectures emerge.

Several directions for future work follow naturally from this study.
First, the UNet-vs-non-UNet generalisation asymmetry motivates
architecturally balanced training strategies that explicitly
oversample rare backbone families.
Second, frequency-domain detection methods~\cite{corvi2023detection},
which directly exploit the spectral profiles that underlie the
artefact distinctiveness identified here, should be evaluated on
\textbf{\datasetname} as a bias-free benchmark.
Third, proactive watermark-based detection~\cite{jiang2024passive}
provides a natural complement to the passive detection approach
evaluated here and can be fairly assessed only on a dataset where
passive shortcuts are eliminated.
Fourth, extending the pipeline to edit-based and
reference-conditioned synthesis would broaden coverage of the
real-world threat landscape.

The complete dataset, eleven-notebook build pipeline, all baseline
checkpoints, and validation report are released at
\url{https://huggingface.co/datasets/Shanmuk4622/ai-detection-dataset-v2}.
